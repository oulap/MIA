In order to proceed, we also need to choose appropriate basis functions m (x) that work in 2D. A convenient choice is often separable basis functions, which are simply the product of two 1D basis functions (one taking as input x 1 , and the other one x 2 ). If there are M 1 basis functions in the rst (row) direction, and M 2 in the second (column) direction, taking all the combinations yields a total of M = M 1 M 2 basis functions in 2D, given by m (x)= m1 (x 1 ) m2 (x 2 ) (1.11)
